# Older Adults

Sarcopenia is the enemy. So is detraining. So is the assumption that older adults can't train hard. They can, and they should — the evidence on resistance training in the 60+ population is overwhelmingly positive for longevity, independence, fall prevention, and quality of life.

The only reason to train someone differently because they're older is to respect:
1. Recovery is slightly slower (more rest between hard sessions)
2. Joint history may dictate movement choices
3. Bone density and balance need explicit attention
4. The goal shifts from "look great" to "stay capable for the next 30 years"

That's it. Programming is still strength training, with care for the above.

## Hard rules — non-negotiable

1. **Medical clearance required.** Cardiovascular events, falls, and asymptomatic conditions are more common at this age. PAR-Q clearance + recent doctor's visit is mandatory before pushing intensity.
2. **Never assume frailty.** Test before you assume. A 70-year-old who's been active their whole life lifts more than most 30-year-olds.
3. **Falls are the highest-stakes outcome.** Balance work is non-negotiable in every session.
4. **Train for power, not just strength.** Power declines faster than strength with age and matters more for catching falls. Add light, fast movements (med ball throws, fast step-ups, light box jumps if joint-tolerant) for power.
5. **Bone density matters.** Multi-joint, loaded movements drive bone density. Resistance training is the strongest non-pharmacological intervention for osteoporosis.

## What changes from a standard program

| Standard adult | Older adult |
|---|---|
| 4–5 sessions/week | 3 sessions/week (more rest between hard days) |
| Compounds at RPE 8 | Compounds at RPE 7 (a rep further from failure) |
| Light cardio optional | Z2 cardio non-negotiable for cardiovascular health |
| Balance work optional | Balance work in every warm-up |
| Eccentric tempo standard | Slightly longer eccentric (3–4 sec down) |
| Recovery: 48 hrs | Recovery: 48–72 hrs after a hard session |
| 3 sets x 8–12 hypertrophy | 3 sets x 8–12 still works — well |
| Power work optional | Power work mandatory |

## Programming priorities, in order

### 1. Strength

The single most impactful thing for an older adult: lift heavy enough to preserve and build muscle. "Heavy" means challenging, not maximal. RPE 6–8 on compound lifts with proper progression.

Required movements (with regressions):
- Squat pattern: goblet squat → box squat → leg press
- Hinge: trap bar deadlift > conventional (less back stress)
- Push: incline DB press > flat bench (shoulder-friendly)
- Pull: cable row, pull-up assisted, lat pulldown
- Carry: farmer carries (HUGE for grip, posture, spine, conditioning)

### 2. Power

Train fast, light movements 1–2x/week:
- Light medicine ball throws (chest pass, slam)
- Fast step-ups (intent on the up)
- Banded throws or punches
- Hip thrusts with intentional speed on concentric
- Box step-offs with stick landing (NOT box jumps unless cleared)

3 sets of 3–6 reps with maximum intentional speed. Light load. Long rest (2 min).

### 3. Balance

Every session warm-up:
- Single-leg stance, 30 sec each side (eyes open progressing to eyes closed)
- Tandem walk (heel-to-toe), 10 steps each direction
- Star excursion reaches (lunge in 4 directions)
- Single-leg deadlift with light KB or BW

This adds ~5 minutes to the warm-up. Worth it.

### 4. Cardio (Zone 2 priority)

Older adults benefit enormously from Z2 cardio. Target:
- 2–3 sessions/week of 30–45 min
- Walking, biking, swimming, easy elliptical
- Conversational pace ("can talk in full sentences")
- HR roughly 60–70% max

Plus 1 short, mild interval session if fitness allows: 4–6 x 1-min hard / 2-min easy on bike.

### 5. Mobility and joint maintenance

End every session with 5–10 min of:
- Hip mobility (90/90, couch stretch with care)
- Thoracic spine extension
- Shoulder mobility (wall slides, banded distractions)
- Ankle mobility (wall ankle dorsiflexion)

These are not optional. Joint range of motion preserves function.

## A 3-day older adult template

**Day A: Squat focus**
- Warm-up (10 min: bike + balance + dynamic mobility)
- Goblet squat 3x8 RPE 6–7
- Trap bar deadlift 3x6 RPE 6–7
- Push-ups (incline if needed) 3x8–12
- Med ball chest pass 3x5 (power)
- Single-leg balance 2x30 sec/side
- Cool-down (5 min mobility)

**Day B: Hinge / pull focus**
- Warm-up
- RDL 3x8 RPE 6–7
- Cable row 3x10
- Pull-up assisted or lat pulldown 3x8
- Step-up with intent 3x5 each leg (power)
- Farmer carry 3x40 sec
- Cool-down

**Day C: Mixed**
- Warm-up
- Goblet squat 3x6 (heavier)
- Incline DB press 3x10
- Bent-over row 3x10
- Side step with band 3x10/side (lateral hip strength)
- Plank 3x30 sec
- Cool-down

**Plus:** 2x Z2 walks/bike rides of 30–45 min on off days.

**Progression:** add 2.5–5 lb on compounds when top of rep range hit at RPE ≤ 7. No rush.

## Special considerations

### Osteoporosis or osteopenia

- Avoid spinal flexion under load (no rounded-back rows, no Russian twists with weight)
- Emphasize deadlift (with care), squat, walking with weight
- Impact loading is good for bone if joints tolerate (light step-down, brisk walking, mild jogging)
- Coordinate with physician on medications (bisphosphonates, etc.)

### Cardiovascular conditions

- Get specific clearance — what's the cap on heart rate? Any meds (beta blockers) that change HR response?
- Warm up longer (10–15 min)
- Build conditioning gradually
- Watch for signs: chest pain, dizziness, severe SOB → stop, evaluate

### Joint replacements (hip, knee)

- Layer in `post-knee-surgery` if recent
- Long-term: train around the joint with appropriate movement choices

### Fall history

- Increase balance work intensity (single-leg with eyes closed, perturbation drills)
- Include reactive movements (stepping out of stance to catch yourself)
- Strength of glute med, ankle stabilizers is critical

### Cognitive decline / dementia

- Simpler programming, repeated patterns
- Coach should be present (don't solo-train)
- Consistency over progression

## The motivation framing

Older adults are often dismissed or babied. Don't. They came to a coach for a reason — they want to be capable. Speak to that.

> "Strength training is the closest thing to a fountain of youth that exists. We're going to keep you strong, balanced, and capable so you can do everything you want to do for the next 30 years. You're not too old to lift hard — you're old enough to know to lift smart. Let's get to work."

## What this skill does NOT do

- Does not patronize or assume the client is fragile
- Does not skip strength work in favor of "senior fitness" pablum
- Does not ignore medical clearance requirements
- Does not assume all older adults are the same — a 65-year-old marathoner and an 80-year-old who hasn't trained in decades are entirely different program builds
