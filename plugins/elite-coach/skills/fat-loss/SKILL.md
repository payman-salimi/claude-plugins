# Fat Loss

The mistake most fat-loss programs make: they turn into cardio programs and abandon strength. The client loses weight — and a lot of it is muscle. They end up "skinny fat" with worse body composition than they started.

Do this instead: **train for strength, eat for fat loss.** The deficit comes from food and a moderate dose of cardio. Strength training preserves the muscle that makes the lower body weight actually look better.

## The non-negotiable principles

1. **Strength training preserves muscle in a deficit.** Drop it and the scale goes down but the body looks worse.
2. **Protein is the most important macro in a cut.** 2.0–2.5 g/kg bodyweight per day. Spread across the day.
3. **Cardio is a tool for the deficit, not a punishment.** Strategic, not unlimited.
4. **Sleep, stress, and adherence beat training and macros.** Most fat-loss failures are adherence, not metabolism.
5. **The deficit should be moderate.** 0.5–1.0% bodyweight per week is the sustainable rate. Faster than that = muscle loss + rebound risk.

## Calorie deficit math (high level — refer to RD or nutrition-coach for precision)

- Maintenance: bodyweight (kg) × 30–35 kcal for moderate activity
- Aggressive cut (advanced lifter, low BF%): 25–30% deficit (e.g., 750 cal off maintenance)
- Standard cut: 15–20% deficit (e.g., 400–500 cal off maintenance)
- Mini cut (4–6 weeks): 25–30% deficit, then return to maintenance
- Slow / sustainable cut (12–20 weeks): 10–15% deficit

**Refer to a Registered Dietitian or nutrition-coach skill for precise macro setting.** This skill prescribes training, not nutrition specifics.

## Training frequency

3–5 days/week strength training is ideal. The temptation in a cut is to add more cardio days at the expense of lifting. Don't. The lifting is the muscle preservation.

## Strength training in a cut: maintain, don't push

In a cut, the goal of strength training is **muscle retention**, not aggressive overload. Most programs should:

- Keep volume moderate (10–15 hard sets/muscle/week — not the 18–22 of a hypertrophy phase)
- Keep intensity high enough to maintain (RPE 7–8 on compounds — same loads, slightly fewer total sets)
- Avoid pushing PRs deep into a cut — it eats recovery you don't have
- Maintain frequency (don't drop sessions to "save energy" — drop volume per session instead)

Translate to a 4-day program:

**Day 1 — Upper**
- Bench press 3x6 @ RPE 7
- Bent-over row 3x6
- Incline DB press 3x8
- Lat pulldown 3x10
- Lateral raise 2x12
- Tricep pushdown 2x10

**Day 2 — Lower**
- Squat 3x6 @ RPE 7
- RDL 3x8
- Leg press 2x10
- Leg curl 2x12
- Calf raise 3x12

**Day 3 — Cardio (Z2 or moderate intervals)**

**Day 4 — Upper**
- Incline press 3x8
- Pull-up 3x6–10
- DB shoulder press 3x8
- Cable row 3x10
- Curl 2x12
- Skull crusher 2x10

**Day 5 — Lower**
- Front squat 3x8
- Hip thrust 3x10
- Lunge 2x10/leg
- Leg curl 2x12

**Day 6 — Cardio (Z2)**

**Day 7 — Rest**

This is fewer total sets than a hypertrophy block, but enough to preserve muscle. Compounds stay loaded.

## Cardio prescription for fat loss

Cardio adds to the deficit without crushing recovery if dosed right.

### Mix to use

- **Zone 2 (steady, conversational)** — 60–70% max HR, can talk in full sentences. Builds aerobic base, low recovery cost. 30–60 min per session.
- **Zone 4 intervals** — 4–6 min hard / 2–3 min easy. Higher cal burn per minute, higher recovery cost. 20–30 min total.
- **Walking** — the underrated tool. 10,000–12,000 steps/day baseline can drive 200–400 cal/day at zero recovery cost.

### Weekly target

- 3 cardio sessions/week (60–90 min total)
- Composition: 2 Z2 + 1 interval session
- Plus daily walking baseline

### Don't overdo HIIT

HIIT is efficient but expensive. Two HIIT sessions/week is the cap before it impairs lifting recovery. Z2 and walking are the safer volume builders.

### Concurrent training: keep cardio separated from lifting when possible

Lifting and cardio in the same session = lifting first, cardio after. Even better: separate sessions by 6+ hours, or different days.

## Tracking progress

Weekly metrics:
- Bodyweight (5–7 day average, not single weigh-in)
- Tape measurements: waist, hip, chest, arms (every 2 weeks)
- Photos (front, side, back, weekly)
- Strength on main lifts (preserve = good; gaining = better)
- Energy level (1–10) and hunger (1–10)

If bodyweight isn't trending down at ~0.5–1% per week:
- Check actual food intake (most clients underestimate by 20–30%)
- Check NEAT (steps may have dropped)
- Check sleep and stress (cortisol drives water retention)
- Adjust deficit slightly, not drastically

If strength is dropping fast: deficit is too steep. Pull back.

## Refeed days and diet breaks

For longer cuts (8+ weeks):
- **Refeed days** every 1–2 weeks: bring calories to maintenance for 1 day, mostly added carbs. Helps thyroid, leptin, mood, training output.
- **Diet breaks** every 4–8 weeks: 1 week at maintenance to reset metabolic and psychological state. Does not undo progress when used right.

## When the cut is over

Cuts have an end. Define it up front:
- Target body fat % or weight
- Target date
- What happens after (transition to maintenance, then potentially a building phase)

Don't cut indefinitely. Reverse out of a cut by adding ~100 cal/week back to maintenance, then sit at maintenance for 4–8 weeks before any new phase.

## Special cases

### Beginners

True novices can lose fat AND build muscle simultaneously (the "newbie gains" window). Run hypertrophy programming with a slight deficit (10–15%) — they get both.

### Advanced / lean clients

Closer to genetic muscle ceiling = harder to preserve in a cut. Keep cuts shorter and more aggressive (mini cut model: 4–6 weeks at 25% deficit, then back to maintenance for several weeks).

### Insulin resistance / metabolic syndrome

Lower-carb biases may help. Refer to MD or RD for medical-grade guidance. Strength training itself is highly therapeutic for insulin sensitivity.

### Postpartum (breastfeeding)

Cap deficit at 250–300 cal. Aggressive cuts can drop milk supply. Read `prenatal-postpartum`.

### Older adults

Cut at slower rate (0.25–0.5% bodyweight/week). Protein floor higher (2.2 g/kg). Read `older-adults`.

## Coaching the client through a cut

Cuts are mentally hard. Build accountability:
- Pre-set the duration: "12 weeks. We'll review at week 6."
- Plan for travel, social events, holidays — give them a "maintenance day" framework
- Manage hunger: high-volume foods (vegetables, lean protein), water, fiber
- Manage sleep: sleep loss = hunger, low willpower, poor recovery

When they hit a stall:
- Don't panic or aggressively cut more
- Run `weekly-checkin`: probably actual intake, NEAT, sleep, or stress
- Hold or adjust slightly. Cuts don't lose linearly.

## Communication to the client

> "Two things matter here: keep your strength, keep showing up. The scale will follow if your food, your protein, your sleep, and your steps line up. We're not turning this into a cardio grind — strength training is what makes the body composition actually look good when the weight comes off. Trust the process. We check in weekly, we adjust if we need to."

- Does not abandon strength work for cardio
- Does not work alongside hypertrophy goals at full intensity (use `body-recomp` if both are wanted)
