# Injury Prep

Train people with bodies that already hurt. The job here is to keep them training around the issue, not to diagnose or fix the issue. That's what a physical therapist is for. Make that disclaimer once, clearly, then get to work.

## Hard rules — non-negotiable

1. **Pain that radiates, is sharp, or is worsening = stop and refer to PT/physician.** Do not program around new sharp pain.
2. **Pain ≤ 3/10 during a movement is workable.** Pain 4–6/10 = regress the movement. Pain 7+ = remove it from the program.
3. **Never train through joint pain on the joint that's flagged.** Use the contralateral side, work above/below the joint, or work the opposite movement pattern.
4. Always start with the *most regressed* version of a movement and progress only when pain stays at or below baseline through the next session.

## The four common patterns and how to train around them

### Low back pain (non-radicular, non-disc)

**Likely culprits**: poor hip hinge mechanics, weak glutes/posterior chain, dominant lumbar extensors, prolonged sitting.

**Avoid for now:**
- Loaded spinal flexion (bent-over rows with rounded back, jefferson curls)
- Heavy unilateral loaded work that requires anti-rotation under fatigue (single-arm overhead carries until cleared)
- Deadlifts from the floor at high % until hinge pattern is locked

**Use:**
- Front squats > back squats (less spinal compression)
- Trap bar deadlifts > conventional
- Rack pulls (knee height) > floor pulls
- Hip thrusts, glute bridges
- Goblet squats, split squats
- Pull-ups, chest-supported rows (no spinal load)
- Carries (suitcase, farmers — light, focus on bracing)

**Prehab to add 2–3x/week:**
- Cat-cow, 8 reps
- Bird dog, 2x10
- Dead bug, 2x10
- Glute bridge, 2x12
- 90/90 hip rotations, 8/side

**Loading rule**: drop intensity to RPE 6–7 on anything spinal-loaded for 2–3 weeks. Only progress if the back stays quiet.

### Knee pain (patellofemoral / general)

**Likely culprits**: weak glutes, weak VMO, tight hip flexors, poor knee tracking.

**Avoid for now:**
- Deep loaded squats below parallel
- Unilateral loaded work with knee tracking issues (lunges if knee caves)
- Box jumps, plyometrics until pain-free in slow patterns
- Leg extension at end-range (terminal knee extension) under load

**Use:**
- Box squats to a target depth that's pain-free
- Step-ups (focus on driving through heel)
- Glute bridges, hip thrusts
- Reverse lunges (less knee shear than forward)
- Spanish squats (band-resisted) — gold standard for patellar tendon
- Leg curls (hamstring strength reduces knee shear)

**Prehab 2–3x/week:**
- Spanish squat 3x10
- Side-lying clamshell 2x15/side
- Single-leg glute bridge 2x10/side
- Terminal knee extensions with band (if pain-free) 2x15
- Calf raises 2x15

**Cardio swap**: rower or bike instead of running until pain-free walking up stairs.

### Shoulder issues (impingement, AC pain, mild rotator cuff)

**Likely culprits**: tight pecs, weak external rotators, poor scap upward rotation, overhead pressing without earned mobility.

**Avoid for now:**
- Overhead barbell pressing
- Upright rows
- Behind-the-neck pulldowns or presses
- Bench press at >90° elbow flare
- Dips deeper than parallel

**Use:**
- Landmine press (semi-vertical, joint-friendly)
- Neutral-grip dumbbell press (palms facing each other, low bench angle)
- Cable row with neutral grip
- Pull-ups with chin-up (supinated) or neutral grip
- Push-ups with proper scap mechanics

**Prehab 3–4x/week:**
- Band external rotation 2x12/side
- Face pulls 2x15
- Y-T-W raises 2x10 each
- Wall slides 2x10
- Scap pull-ups 2x8

**Loading rule**: any pressing stays at RPE 6–7 with full ROM for 4 weeks before pushing intensity.

### Hip pain / impingement

**Avoid for now:**
- Deep front squats (if hip flexion produces pinch)
- Sumo deadlifts at wide stance
- Hip flexor heavy work (leg raises)

**Use:**
- Hip thrusts
- Reverse lunges, split squats with shorter range
- Trap bar deadlift narrow stance
- Cable pull-throughs

**Prehab:**
- 90/90 stretch
- Couch stretch (1 min/side)
- Banded hip distractions
- Glute med work (clamshells, side planks with hip abduction)

## How to integrate into program-design

When `program-design` is building the workout:
1. First, scan the client's reported issues
2. Cross-reference against the patterns above
3. Replace any banned movement with the listed alternative
4. Insert the relevant prehab block as the warm-up
5. Cap intensity per the loading rule for that pattern
6. Add a re-evaluation note: "Re-check pain at week 3. If quiet, progress the movement."

## Communication to the client

Always say it once, clearly:

> "I'm going to design around your [back / knee / shoulder]. This isn't a diagnosis — for that, see a PT. What I can do is keep you training while you sort out the underlying issue. Two rules: pain over 4/10 during a lift, we modify it. Pain that radiates or gets sharper, you stop and message your doctor."

Then build the program.

## Progression criteria

A movement progresses (more load, deeper range, less regression) when:
- Pain stays ≤ baseline during the movement for 2 sessions in a row
- The next regression up has been performed pain-free
- Recovery between sessions doesn't show new aggravation

Otherwise hold.

## What this skill does NOT do

- Does not diagnose
- Does not prescribe physical therapy exercises beyond standard prehab
- Does not replace a real medical workup
- Does not work for post-surgical clients (use `post-knee-surgery` for those)
