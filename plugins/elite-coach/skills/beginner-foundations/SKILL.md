# Beginner Foundations

The most common reason new lifters quit is overwhelm. The second is injury from doing too much too soon. Both are programming problems. Build a plan they can actually do, that gets them stronger fast, and that doesn't require a PhD to follow.

## The novice opportunity

True novices have a once-in-a-lifetime advantage: **linear progression works for months**. Every session, more weight on the bar. This window closes — but for now, capture it. Don't waste it on fancy splits and accessory soup. The big lifts are the program.

## Programming principles

### 1. Frequency: 3 sessions / week, full body

Not push/pull/legs. Not bro split. Not 5x/week. Three full-body sessions, separated by a rest day. This wins because:
- High frequency on the big lifts drives skill acquisition
- 3 days is sustainable for almost anyone
- Recovery windows are simple and predictable

### 2. The lifts: 5 patterns, every session

Every workout hits all five patterns:

1. **Squat pattern** (squat, goblet squat, leg press)
2. **Hinge pattern** (deadlift variation, RDL, hip thrust)
3. **Push pattern** (bench press, dumbbell press, push-up)
4. **Pull pattern** (row, pull-up, lat pulldown)
5. **Carry / core** (farmer carry, plank, dead bug)

Pick ONE variation per pattern per session. Don't add 3 chest exercises. Five lifts, total. Maybe 1–2 small accessories at the end if energy allows.

### 3. Sets and reps: simple

For the first 8–12 weeks, run **3 sets of 5 reps** for compound lifts (or 3x8–10 if their joints don't tolerate heavy 5s yet). Accessories at 2–3x10–12.

Why 3x5: sweet spot of strength and skill. Why not 5x5 right away: too much volume early; recovery is the limiter.

### 4. Linear progression — the magic

After every successful session, **add weight** to the lift:

- Compound barbell lifts: +5 lb each session (squat/bench/press) or +10 lb (deadlift)
- Dumbbell variations: smallest jump available (often +5 lb total = +2.5 per dumbbell)
- Accessories: when top of rep range hit at all sets, add weight

When they fail to hit 3x5 at a given weight: re-attempt next session. Fail twice in a row: drop 10% and ramp back. This will happen. It's not a setback — it's how progression works.

### 5. Warm-up: short and reliable

5 min of:
- 1–2 min easy cardio (bike, walk, rower)
- Dynamic warm-up: 5 each of leg swings, arm circles, walking lunges, world's greatest stretch
- Lift-specific warm-up sets (bar, then 50%, 70%, 90% of working weight x 3–5 reps)

That's the warm-up. Not a 25-minute mobility routine — they don't need it yet, and it eats into time they could spend lifting.

### 6. No cardio interference (yet)

For the first 8 weeks, optional 1–2 walks of 30 minutes/week. That's it. Don't pile cardio on a beginner who's already adapting to lifting — recovery is the bottleneck. Cardio comes back in once strength is moving consistently.

### 7. Session length: 45–60 min

A novice does not need 90-minute sessions. Three lifts at 3x5 with proper rest (2–3 min between sets on compounds) plus 1–2 accessories fits in 45 min comfortably.

### 8. Tracking: the most important habit

Write everything down. Every set. Every rep. Every weight. The .xlsx tracker from `program-design` is for this. The act of tracking is what makes linear progression work — they can see exactly what to lift today.

## Common beginner pitfalls — get ahead of them

### Pitfall: chasing programs they saw online

Bro splits, RP templates, peaking blocks. Tell them: **none of that is for you yet**. Six to twelve months of consistent linear progression will outproduce any fancy program they could attempt now. Save the complexity for when their body has earned it.

### Pitfall: ego on weight

Beginners often try to lift more than their form allows. Cap it at form. Cue:
- Squat: depth at parallel or below, knees track over toes, neutral spine
- Deadlift: bar drags up the legs, hips and shoulders rise together, no rounding
- Bench: feet planted, scaps retracted, bar to chest, no bouncing
- Press: ribs down, glutes squeezed, full lockout overhead

If form breaks at a weight, that's the cap until form holds.

### Pitfall: too much, too soon

Some beginners want to train 6 days a week immediately. Push back. Three days, done well, beats six days half-assed. Adherence > frequency for novices.

### Pitfall: missing sessions and starting over

Two missed sessions doesn't mean restarting from zero. Re-enter the program at the last successful working weight, not the original starting weight.

### Pitfall: not eating enough

Strength training without enough food = no progress. Tell them:
- Eat protein at every meal: 0.8–1g per lb bodyweight per day target
- Don't try to lose weight aggressively in the first 12 weeks — focus on getting strong
- Sleep 7+ hours

### Pitfall: confused by gym etiquette / equipment

Spend the first session walking through the gym. Show them:
- How to load a barbell
- How to use safety pins / spotter arms
- How to ask to work in
- How not to be the weirdo blocking the squat rack with curls

This reduces the intimidation that makes them not show up.

## The 12-week beginner template

**Sessions:** 3x/week (e.g., Mon/Wed/Fri)

**Workout A:**
- Squat 3x5
- Bench press 3x5
- Bent-over row 3x5
- Plank 3x30 sec
- (optional) Bicep curls 2x10

**Workout B:**
- Deadlift 1x5 (or trap bar deadlift 3x5 if conventional is too advanced)
- Overhead press 3x5
- Lat pulldown or pull-up 3x6–10
- Dead bug 3x10
- (optional) Tricep pushdown 2x10

**Schedule:** A, B, A one week. B, A, B the next. (Alternating because deadlifts only happen 1x/week — they're heavy and recovery-intensive.)

**Progression:** add weight every session that all sets x reps were completed. Stall twice in a row → deload 10%, ramp back.

**Cardio:** 1–2 walks of 30 min/week.

**Deload:** week 8 if needed (50% volume, 70% intensity for one week).

After 12 weeks, reassess. Move to an intermediate template (4-day upper/lower or push/pull/legs) only when linear progression has clearly stalled across multiple lifts.

## Communication to the client

> "I want you to leave here in 6 months looking back at today and not recognizing yourself. The plan is simple — three days a week, five lifts, add weight every session. Track everything. Show up. The progress you're going to see in the first three months is the best return on training time you'll ever get in your life. Don't waste it on a complicated plan."

## What this skill does NOT do

- Does not run elaborate splits for novices
- Does not recommend supplements (basic nutrition only — protein, sleep, food adequacy)
- Does not prescribe heavy cardio in the first weeks
- Does not assume "beginner" = "weak" — beginners can lift heavier than they think, just safely and with progression
