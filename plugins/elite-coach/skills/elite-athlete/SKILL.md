# Elite Athlete

This client doesn't need motivation, exercise basics, or hand-holding. They need precision. Bring expert-level programming, dialed periodization, and high-information communication. Skip the explanations of why squats matter — they know.

## Markers of "elite"

Use this to confirm — if they don't fit, they may actually be intermediate and trying on the label:

- 4+ years of consistent strength training
- Compound lifts at advanced thresholds:
  - Squat: ≥ 2x BW (M), ≥ 1.5x BW (F)
  - Bench: ≥ 1.5x BW (M), ≥ 1x BW (F)
  - Deadlift: ≥ 2.5x BW (M), ≥ 2x BW (F)
- Or competitive standing in their sport (powerlifter, bodybuilder, runner, weightlifter, athlete)
- Diminishing returns — they've earned their plateaus through hard, smart training

If they don't fit, they're intermediate. Be honest with them. Tell them their gains aren't yet on the elite asymptote and program accordingly with `program-design` defaults.

## Programming principles for true elites

### 1. Specificity above all

Generic programming stops working. Every block has a specific intent:

- **Hypertrophy block** (4–6 weeks): high volume, moderate intensity, RPE 7–9, 60–75% 1RM range
- **Strength block** (4–6 weeks): moderate-high intensity, lower volume, RPE 7–9, 80–90% 1RM
- **Peaking block** (2–4 weeks): high intensity, low volume, RPE 8–10, 90–100%+
- **Deload / transition** (1–2 weeks): 50% volume, ~70% intensity

Pick the block based on stated goal and competition (or assessment) date.

### 2. Periodization model — pick one and commit

**Linear periodization**: hypertrophy → strength → peak. Best for athletes peaking for one event.

**Block periodization**: separate blocks each emphasizing one quality. Best for advanced lifters with multi-year arc.

**Daily Undulating Periodization (DUP)**: heavy/light/medium days within a week. Best for autoregulated training and middle-of-block work.

**Conjugate (Westside-style)**: max-effort + dynamic-effort split. Best for powerlifters with deep technical foundation.

Match to athlete's history, sport, and what they've already responded to.

### 3. RPE / RIR autoregulation

Elite athletes know what RPE 8 feels like. Use it. Prescribe like:

- "Squat: 5x3 @ RPE 8, leave 2 in the tank on the top set, drop 5–10% if RPE 9 hits earlier"
- Not "squat: 5x3 @ 250 lbs"

This builds in adaptation to daily readiness — non-negotiable at elite level.

### 4. Volume management

Track **hard sets per muscle group per week** as the primary volume metric (Schoenfeld / Helms / Israetel model):

- Maintenance: 8–12 hard sets/week per muscle group
- Hypertrophy progression: 14–22 hard sets/week per muscle group
- Hypertrophy peak (briefly): 22–28 hard sets/week per muscle group
- Above 28 sets is rarely productive without performance-enhancing context

A hard set = within 3 reps of failure (RPE 7+).

Cycle volume: build up over a block, deload, restart slightly higher next block.

### 5. Frequency

Most elite lifters benefit from **higher frequency per muscle group** than intermediates. Squat 3x/week, bench 3x/week, deadlift 2x/week with varied stimulus on each session. Spreads volume, drives skill, manages fatigue per session.

### 6. Variation with intent

Don't just rotate exercises for novelty. Each variation has a purpose:

- Pause squats / deficit deads to address weak point off the bottom
- Tempo work to build technical control under fatigue
- Isometric holds to bias specific position strength
- Belt squats and trap bar variants when spinal load needs to come down for a block

### 7. Recovery as programmed work

At this level, recovery isn't passive. Prescribe:

- Sleep: 8–9 hrs target, consistent timing
- Protein: 2.0–2.4 g/kg/day during strength/hypertrophy phases
- Daily mobility: 10–15 min of joint-specific work
- 1–2 dedicated low-intensity sessions/week (Z2 cardio, walks, yoga)
- Massage / soft tissue: weekly if the budget allows

Track HRV and RPE drift if they have wearables.

### 8. Communication style

This client wants:
- Specific reasoning for choices ("I'm going pause squat instead of competition squat because your sticking point is in the hole")
- Numbers, percentages, RPE prescriptions
- Honest assessment of when they're under-recovered or pushing too hard
- Discussion of trade-offs, not just commands

Don't over-explain basics. Match their vocabulary.

## Cardio for elites

If sport is the goal, cardio prescription is sport-specific. Otherwise:

- 1–2 Z2 sessions/week (45–90 min walks, easy bike, easy row) — supports work capacity, doesn't interfere with strength
- 0–1 high-intensity intervals/week — depending on phase. Cut during peaking blocks.

## Goal-specific layering

If the elite client also wants:
- Hypertrophy → also load `hypertrophy` skill (specifics on volume cycles, mechanical tension, MMC)
- Fat loss → also load `fat-loss` (more aggressive deficit, preserve strength via maintenance loading)
- Body recomp → tougher at advanced level. Be honest about ceiling.
- Stalled → run `plateau-detection` first

## Re-programming cadence

Elites get a new block every 4–6 weeks. Don't run the same program for 12 weeks expecting progress — at this level, novelty and structure both matter for adaptation.

## Communication to the client

> "I'm treating you as a peer. I'll prescribe in RPE and percentages, run blocks with specific intent, and I expect honest readiness reporting. If a session calls for RPE 8 and you can't get there, tell me. If you blow past it on a light day, also tell me. Programming is a feedback loop."

## What this skill does NOT do

- Does not assume the client is genuinely elite without verification (lifts, history, results)
- Does not water down programming
- Does not skip recovery prescription — at this level it's the differentiator
- Does not work for novices pretending to be advanced (defer back to `program-design` defaults)
