# Post-Knee Surgery

Returning a client to training after knee surgery is **a partnership with their physical therapist, not a replacement for one**. Your job is to bridge from PT clinic exercises to real strength and conditioning while respecting the surgical timeline. Never override the PT.

## Hard rules — non-negotiable

1. **No programming without PT clearance.** Period. If the client has not been formally discharged or cleared for strength work, refer them back to their PT.
2. **Surgical timelines control everything.** Tissue healing has a biological clock. ACL grafts take 9–12 months minimum to remodel. Meniscus repairs can take 4–6 months. Don't rush biology.
3. **Bilateral asymmetry > 10% (operated leg vs. non-operated) on a single-leg movement = not ready to progress.**
4. **Pain, swelling, or instability after a session = regress.** These are the body telling you the dose was too high.
5. **Always train the non-operated leg.** Cross-education effect maintains strength on the operated side via central neural adaptation. Train it heavy.

## Surgery-by-surgery framework

### ACL reconstruction

The big one. Timeline-driven.

**Weeks 0–6 (PT phase 1 — coach not involved yet):**
- Quad activation, ROM restoration, swelling control. Done in clinic.
- Coach does not program here.

**Weeks 6–12 (PT phase 2 — coach in coordination):**
- Continue PT. Coach can layer in unaffected upper-body and core work.
- Bilateral squats with no load to body weight, partial ROM, only with PT clearance
- No single-leg loading on the operated side
- Stationary bike, no resistance, building duration

**Weeks 12–20 (PT phase 3 — strength rebuilding):**
- Bilateral squats progressing to goblet squats
- Leg press (bilateral, controlled ROM)
- Leg curls (hamstring strength is critical for ACL)
- Step-ups starting low (4–6 inch box)
- Single-leg work on non-operated leg only at full intensity
- Begin light single-leg work on operated side (banded leg extensions, single-leg leg press at low weight)

**Months 5–9 (return-to-running phase):**
- Begin running progressions only with PT/MD clearance and quad strength benchmark (operated quad ≥ 80% of non-operated)
- Lateral movements introduced
- Plyometrics start: low-impact double-leg → low-impact single-leg
- Strength work continues: full ROM bilateral, progressing single-leg

**Months 9–12 (return-to-sport phase):**
- Full strength training with caution on max-effort single-leg work
- Sport-specific training begins
- Cutting, deceleration, jumping in controlled progressions
- **No return to cutting sports without RTS testing (hop tests, IKDC, etc.)** — that's PT/MD's call

### Meniscus repair vs. meniscectomy

**Meniscectomy (partial removal)**: faster timeline. Most can return to lifting within 6–8 weeks with PT clearance. Avoid deep squats and high-impact loading initially.

**Meniscus repair**: slower. The meniscus is being preserved and needs to heal. 4–6 months before return to lifting. **No deep squatting, no twisting under load, no impact for 4 months minimum.**

**For both:**
- Bilateral squats only initially, progressing to single-leg at month 3 (repair) or week 6 (meniscectomy)
- Bike, swim, elliptical for cardio
- Avoid running until cleared
- Watch for swelling — that's the meniscus complaining

### Total Knee Replacement (TKR)

**Older client population, often.** Goal: restore independence and basic strength. Don't think of this client as a returning athlete — think of them as rebuilding capacity.

**Months 0–3:** PT only. Coach not involved.

**Months 3–6:**
- Bilateral, supported movements: chair sit-to-stand, partial squats, glute bridges, leg press at low weight
- Build ROM and quad strength
- Stationary bike (no resistance, then light) for ROM and circulation
- Step-ups (low box)
- No running, no jumping

**Months 6–12:**
- Progress load on bilateral movements
- Add single-leg progressions slowly
- Walking → power walking
- No high-impact for at least 12 months — the prosthesis benefits from low-impact loading

**Lifelong:**
- Maintain quad and glute strength
- Keep movement varied
- Most TKR patients should not run regularly — bike, walk, swim, row instead

### Patellar tendon repair

- Conservative timeline. ROM restoration first.
- 4–6 months before progressive loading
- Spanish squats and isometric quad work are gold-standard for tendon remodeling
- No deep loaded squatting until 6+ months and PT clearance
- Slow, isometric → eccentric → concentric progressions

### MCL/LCL repair (or sprain Grade 2–3)

- Avoid valgus/varus stress (lateral load on knee) for the first 4–6 weeks
- Bilateral movements only initially
- Single-leg work re-introduced once stability is restored

## Movement substitutions for any post-knee return

| Banned (until cleared) | Substitute |
|---|---|
| Conventional squat | Goblet squat / box squat to comfortable depth |
| Deep squat | Box squat to parallel or above |
| Lunges (forward) | Reverse lunges or split squats |
| Single-leg deadlift | RDL (bilateral) |
| Box jumps / plyos | Step-ups, sled push |
| Running | Stationary bike, swimming, rower |
| Pivoting / cutting | Linear movement only |

## Loading rule

Cap RPE at 6–7 on any knee-loaded movement until 6 months post-op (ACL/meniscus repair) or 4 months (meniscectomy/MCL). Single-leg work on operated side is even more conservative.

Use percentage of non-operated leg's strength as a benchmark:
- < 70%: bilateral only, focus on rebuild
- 70–85%: introduce light single-leg
- 85%+: progress single-leg load
- 95%+: ready for return-to-sport considerations

## How to integrate into program-design

1. Confirm: which surgery, what date, surgeon's protocol, current PT status
2. Identify the phase per the surgery type
3. Replace banned movements with the substitution table
4. Cap intensity per phase
5. Always include the non-operated leg at full intensity (cross-education benefit)
6. Add quad and glute activation work as warm-up
7. Schedule re-evaluation aligned with PT discharge or check-in dates

## Cardio prescription

Stationary bike is your best friend for the first 3–6 months post-op for any knee surgery. Builds ROM, circulation, conditioning, no impact. Add rower or elliptical once cleared. Running comes last.

## Communication to the client

> "We're going to rebuild this knee carefully. Your PT calls the shots on what you're cleared for — I program around their progressions. Two things from you: (1) tell me anything your PT says, every appointment. (2) Tell me about swelling, pain, or instability after sessions. Those are signals, not setbacks. We'll get you back, and we'll get you back stronger than the surgery left you."

## Long-term goal

Most ACL clients can return to full strength training and even sport by 9–12 months. Most meniscus and TKR clients can return to consistent strength work indefinitely with care. The operated knee should not be a permanent fragility — it should be a permanent reminder to train smart.

## What this skill does NOT do

- Does not replace the surgeon or PT
- Does not run the early post-op phase (that's PT's job)
- Does not skip biological healing timelines under any pressure
- Does not assume all knee surgeries are the same — each has its own timeline
