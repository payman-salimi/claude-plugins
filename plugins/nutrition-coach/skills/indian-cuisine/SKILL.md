# Indian cuisine

Indian food is one of the most spice-complex cuisines in the world and the most well-developed vegetarian cooking tradition anywhere. It varies enormously by region — North Indian (heavier, dairy-rich, wheat-based) is very different from South Indian (rice-based, coconut-rich, often lighter). Tell the user this upfront if they say "Indian food" generically — ask which region or which dishes they grew up with.

## Core spice palette

**Whole spices**
- Cumin seeds (jeera), mustard seeds (rai), fennel seeds (saunf)
- Coriander seeds (dhania), black pepper, cardamom (green and black)
- Cloves, cinnamon stick, bay leaf, nutmeg, mace
- Red chilies (whole dry, e.g., Kashmiri or Byadgi for color without heat)
- Curry leaves, asafoetida (hing)

**Ground spices**
- Turmeric (haldi), ground coriander, ground cumin, red chili powder
- Garam masala (a finishing spice blend), kasuri methi (dried fenugreek leaves)
- Chaat masala (a sour-savory finisher), amchur (dried mango powder)

**Fresh aromatics**
- Ginger (adrak), garlic (lehsun), green chili
- Cilantro (dhania) — used in volume, finishing herb
- Mint (pudina), curry leaves (kadi patta)

## Proteins

- **Vegetarian (the strength of Indian cooking)**: dal (split lentils — toor, masoor, chana, urad, moong), chickpeas (chana), kidney beans (rajma), black-eyed peas, paneer
- **Eggs** — common in Bengali and South Indian
- **Chicken** — central to Punjabi/North Indian (butter chicken, tikka, biryani)
- **Lamb / mutton (goat in India)** — North Indian, occasional
- **Fish** — coastal regions (Bengali, Goan, Kerala, Andhra)
- **Shrimp** — coastal
- Beef and pork are uncommon in most Indian regions for religious reasons

## Carbs

- **North**: wheat — chapati/roti, naan, paratha, kulcha
- **South**: rice — plain steamed, dosa (rice + lentil crepe), idli (steamed rice cake), uttapam
- **East/Bengali**: rice central
- **West/Gujarati**: a mix, plus thepla (flatbread)
- **Biryani / pulao**: long-grain basmati, rich, special-occasion

## Fats

- **Ghee** (clarified butter) — the traditional cooking fat in much of India, especially North
- **Mustard oil** — pungent, traditional in Bengali and Punjabi cooking
- **Coconut oil** — South Indian (Kerala) and coastal
- **Neutral vegetable oil** — modern home cooking
- **Cream / milk** — in North Indian curries (use modestly for nutrition goals)

## Signature dishes by region

### North Indian (Punjabi, etc.)
- **Dal makhani** — slow-cooked black lentils + kidney beans in cream
- **Butter chicken (murgh makhani)** — tomato-cream-based chicken curry
- **Chana masala** — chickpeas in spiced tomato gravy
- **Rajma chawal** — kidney bean curry with rice
- **Paneer dishes** — palak paneer, paneer tikka, kadai paneer
- **Tandoori chicken / chicken tikka** — yogurt-marinated, dry-grilled
- **Biryani (Hyderabadi, Lucknowi)** — layered rice + meat, slow-cooked

### South Indian (Tamil, Karnataka, Kerala)
- **Dosa + sambar + chutney** — fermented crepe with lentil stew and coconut chutney
- **Idli + sambar** — steamed rice cakes
- **Sambar** — tamarind-based lentil stew with vegetables
- **Rasam** — thin tamarind-pepper soup
- **Coconut-based curries** — Kerala fish curry, Mangalorean chicken
- **Curd rice (thayir sadam)** — yogurt + rice, summer staple

### Bengali (East)
- **Macher jhol** — fish in light spiced broth
- **Cholar dal** — chana dal with coconut and raisins
- **Aloo posto** — potato in poppy-seed paste
- **Shorshe maach** — fish in mustard sauce

### Gujarati (West, vegetarian-leaning)
- **Dhokla** — steamed chickpea-flour cake
- **Khaman** — softer dhokla variant
- **Undhiyu** — mixed vegetable casserole
- **Khichdi** — rice + lentil one-pot, with kadhi (yogurt curry)

## Technique notes

- **Tadka / tempering** — Heat ghee/oil hot, drop in whole spices (cumin seeds, mustard seeds, dried chilies, curry leaves) and let them sizzle 10–20 seconds. This is poured over dal, soup, or vegetables at the end. It transforms the dish; it is not optional.
- **Bhuna** — Onion + ginger + garlic + tomato cooked down in oil until the oil separates and the mixture turns deep red-brown, ~15 minutes. This is the base of nearly every North Indian curry. Skipping or rushing it is the most common failure point.
- **Layering of spices** — Whole spices go in early (in the oil); ground spices go in mid-cook; garam masala and kasuri methi go in at the end (their volatile oils burn off).
- **Curry leaves** are non-substitutable — the cuisine needs them. Bay leaves are not the same. Buy fresh and freeze.
- **Salt timing** — Add early in the bhuna stage, not at the end. Indian food's depth comes partly from properly salted aromatics.

## Macro patterns

Indian food can run very high in fat (ghee, cream, fried elements) or very lean (dal + roti + sabzi + raita). The plan-builder's job is to bias toward the lean side:
- Dal, sabzi (vegetable), roti, raita = naturally well-balanced
- Add chicken or fish for non-vegetarian protein boost
- Limit deep-fried items (samosa, pakora, puri) to once a week or less
- Use ghee/oil with a measuring spoon — easy place for hidden calories

A balanced Indian plate hits:
- 45–55% carbs (rice, lentils, roti)
- 25–30% fats (ghee, oils, dairy)
- 20–25% protein (lentils, dairy, chicken/fish)

## Suggested weekly anchors

- 1 dal night (toor, masoor, chana, or moong) — naturally vegetarian
- 1 chicken curry night (chana masala can be the side instead)
- 1 fish or shrimp night (coastal-style with coconut, or simple Bengali jhol)
- 1 paneer or vegetarian protein night
- 1 South Indian night (dosa or sambar)
- 1 biryani or pulao (special — make a smaller portion)
- Lunches: leftover dal + rice + raita, or roti + sabzi + chickpeas
- Breakfasts: idli + sambar, paratha + yogurt, or eggs + roti

## What NOT to do

- Do not say "Indian curry" as a generic. There is no such thing — be specific about the dish.
- Do not skip the bhuna and call it Indian food. Quick "curry" recipes that skip this stage taste flat.
- Do not use generic "curry powder." Use individual spices and assemble per dish.
- Do not assume Indian = vegetarian. North Indian food has heavy chicken and lamb traditions; coastal and Bengali food is fish-heavy.
- Do not use cream as a default thickener. Use cashew paste, yogurt, coconut milk, or reduction depending on the region.
- Do not load the plan with butter chicken three times. That dish is delicious and not a daily-driver.
