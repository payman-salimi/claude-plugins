# Asian cuisine

"Asian food" spans many distinct culinary traditions. When a user picks this skill, immediately ask which one(s) they want — Chinese, Japanese, Korean, Thai, Vietnamese, Filipino, Malaysian, Indonesian, or a mix. The differences are real: a Vietnamese pho is built on a long-simmered beef broth with sweet anise notes; a Japanese ramen broth uses pork, miso, or soy and goes a different direction entirely.

## Common ingredient palette across the region

**Umami-builders (the central flavor logic)**
- Soy sauce (Chinese light/dark, Japanese shoyu, Korean ganjang)
- Fish sauce (Vietnamese nuoc mam, Thai nam pla, Filipino patis)
- Miso (Japanese — white/red/awase)
- Doenjang and gochujang (Korean fermented soybean and chili paste)
- Oyster sauce, hoisin (Chinese)
- Dashi (Japanese — kombu + bonito stock)
- Dried shiitake, dried shrimp, dried anchovies (across the region)

**Aromatics**
- Ginger, garlic, scallion (the universal trio)
- Lemongrass (Southeast Asia)
- Galangal (Thai, distinct from ginger — sharper, more pine-like)
- Kaffir lime leaves (Thai)
- Thai bird chilies, Korean gochugaru flake, Sichuan peppercorn
- Cilantro, Thai basil, mint, perilla (shiso)

**Fats**
- Neutral oils (canola, peanut)
- Sesame oil (toasted — finishing only)
- Coconut milk and oil (Southeast Asia, especially Thai/Vietnamese/Indonesian)

**Carbs**
- Rice (jasmine, short-grain Japanese, basmati, sticky/glutinous)
- Noodles: rice noodles, wheat noodles (lo mein, udon, soba, ramen), glass/bean thread, egg noodles
- Wrappers: rice paper (Vietnamese), wonton, dumpling

**Proteins**
- Chicken, pork, beef, duck
- Fish: salmon, tuna, mackerel, white fish; shellfish abundant on coasts
- Tofu and tempeh (Indonesian); fermented soy products
- Eggs (in everything — fried rice, ramen, banchan)
- Pork (central to Chinese, Filipino, Vietnamese; not used in halal contexts)

## Regional patterns

### Chinese (eight major regions)
- **Cantonese** — fresh, lighter, steamed fish, dim sum, char siu
- **Sichuan** — bold chili and Sichuan peppercorn (mala)
- **Shanghainese** — sweeter, soy-braised, xiao long bao
- **Northern (Beijing)** — wheat-based — dumplings, noodles, scallion pancakes
- **Hunan, Yunnan, Fujian, Anhui** — distinct in their own right

### Japanese
- **Donburi** (rice bowls): oyakodon, gyudon, katsudon
- **Ramen** — tonkotsu (pork bone), miso, shoyu, shio
- **Sushi & sashimi** — raw fish over vinegared rice; nigiri, maki, chirashi
- **Tempura** — light battered fry
- **Yakitori** — grilled chicken skewers
- **Teishoku** (set meals): rice + grilled fish + miso soup + tsukemono (pickles) + small vegetable side

### Korean
- **Bibimbap** — rice bowl with assorted vegetables, beef, gochujang, fried egg
- **Bulgogi** — soy-marinated grilled beef
- **Galbi** — marinated short ribs
- **Kimchi jjigae / sundubu jjigae** — kimchi or soft tofu stew
- **Banchan** — small side dishes that accompany every meal (kimchi, namul vegetables, pickles)
- **Korean BBQ** — grill-at-table style with lettuce wraps and ssamjang

### Thai
- **Tom yum / tom kha** — sour-and-spicy / coconut-galangal soups
- **Pad Thai, pad see ew, drunken noodles** — stir-fried noodles
- **Green / red / massaman curry** — coconut-based curries with curry paste base
- **Larb** — minced meat salad with herbs and toasted rice powder
- **Som tum** — green papaya salad

### Vietnamese
- **Pho** — beef or chicken broth with rice noodles
- **Bun cha** — grilled pork over rice noodles with herb plate and dipping sauce
- **Banh mi** — French baguette + Vietnamese fillings
- **Goi cuon** — fresh spring rolls in rice paper
- **Com tam** — broken rice with grilled pork

### Filipino
- **Adobo** — soy + vinegar braised chicken or pork
- **Sinigang** — sour tamarind soup
- **Lumpia** — fried spring rolls
- **Kare-kare** — peanut-based oxtail stew

## Technique notes

- **Wok hei** — the breath of the wok. Achieved with a very hot wok, dry ingredients, fast tossing, small batches. Home stoves rarely match restaurant burners; compensate by cooking in two batches and not crowding the pan.
- **Dashi** — simmer kombu in cold water, remove just before boil; add bonito flakes, steep 5 min, strain. The base of most Japanese soup and braise.
- **Curry pastes (Thai)** — pound or food-process from raw ingredients (lemongrass, galangal, chilies, shrimp paste, garlic, shallot, kaffir lime). Pastes from a jar are acceptable starters but lose freshness fast.
- **Rice cookery** — 1:1 water for jasmine rice (rice cooker default); short-grain Japanese rice needs rinsing until water runs clear, then a 30-min rest before cooking.
- **Toasted sesame oil** is a finishing oil. Cooking with it destroys its aroma — drizzle at the end.
- **Fish sauce** — its smell is alarming; the cooked flavor is not. Use it. Vegetarian substitute: light soy + a pinch of sugar + dried mushroom liquor, but the flavor is different.

## Macro patterns

Asian cuisines vary widely. General balanced patterns:
- Japanese teishoku: 50% carbs (rice), 25% protein (fish), 25% vegetables/fats — naturally lean and balanced
- Chinese stir-fries: easy to manage with brown rice + lean protein + lots of veg + measured oil
- Thai/Vietnamese: rice or noodle + protein + fresh herbs + small amount of nuoc cham/sauce
- Korean: rice + protein + lots of banchan (vegetable-forward) + soup

For fat-loss plans, watch for:
- Hidden cooking oil in stir-fries (often 2–4 tbsp per serving)
- Coconut milk in curries (very calorie-dense)
- Battered or deep-fried items (tempura, fried spring rolls)
- White rice volume — measure portions

## Suggested weekly anchors

- 1 stir-fry night (Chinese-style, lean protein + brown rice)
- 1 soup-noodle night (pho or ramen — make a leaner version, lots of vegetables)
- 1 curry night (Thai green curry with chicken and lots of veg, modest coconut milk)
- 1 grilled-protein night (yakitori, bulgogi, satay) with rice + vegetable
- 1 rice bowl night (Japanese donburi, Korean bibimbap)
- 1 fresh-and-light night (Vietnamese spring rolls + grilled protein, or Thai larb)
- Lunches: cold soba/rice noodle bowls, leftover stir-fry, Korean rice bowls

## What NOT to do

- Do not group all Asian cuisines into "Asian fusion" — pick a tradition.
- Do not over-rely on takeout-style heavy sauces. Authentic Asian cooking is usually lighter than westernized versions.
- Do not skip the rest after rice cooks — short-grain Japanese rice needs 10 minutes of resting covered.
- Do not crowd a stir-fry pan. Two batches in five minutes beats one batch in ten.
- Do not pour soy sauce by eye into rice or noodles. Measure — sodium adds up fast.
- Do not call all chili pastes equivalent. Korean gochujang, Chinese doubanjiang, Thai nam prik pao, Indonesian sambal — each has a distinct flavor and use.
