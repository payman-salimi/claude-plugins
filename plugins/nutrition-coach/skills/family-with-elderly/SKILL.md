# Family with elderly

Older adults are not a monolith. A vigorous 70-year-old who walks 5 km a day may need no plan adjustments at all; a frail 88-year-old recovering from hip surgery may need everything different. Ask before assuming.

## Ask first

Before adjusting the plan:
- **Age and overall health** — independent and active, or has health conditions to manage?
- **Chewing/swallowing** — any dental issues or swallowing concerns (dysphagia)?
- **Appetite** — reduced or normal? Many older adults experience appetite decline.
- **Medical conditions** — diabetes, heart disease, kidney disease, hypertension, osteoporosis, dementia
- **Medications** — many drugs affect nutrition (warfarin and vitamin K, statins and grapefruit, MAOIs and tyramine foods, certain diabetes meds and timing)
- **Cognitive status** — does the person feed themselves? Do they remember to eat?
- **Living situation** — cooking themselves, in a household, or in assisted care?

If the person has dementia, dysphagia, or significant unintended weight loss, recommend a registered dietitian and the primary care provider be involved. The plan can support, not replace, that care.

## Nutrient priorities for older adults

These tend to fall short and matter most:

- **Protein** — 1.0–1.2 g/kg/day minimum; 1.2–1.6 g/kg/day if active or recovering from illness. Older muscles are less responsive to protein (anabolic resistance) — every meal should have a substantial protein source, ideally 25–35 g per meal.
- **Calcium** — 1200 mg/day for women 51+, men 71+. Dairy, leafy greens, fortified plant milks, sardines.
- **Vitamin D** — 600–800 IU/day from food + sun + supplement if recommended. Fatty fish, fortified milk, eggs.
- **Vitamin B12** — absorption decreases with age; many older adults need fortified foods or supplements. Eggs, dairy, meat, fortified cereals.
- **Fiber** — for digestive regularity and cardiovascular health. Whole grains, vegetables, fruit, beans.
- **Hydration** — thirst sensation declines with age. Encourage fluids throughout the day, not just water (soup, fruit, herbal tea, milk all count).
- **Iron** — often a concern for women post-menopause if there are absorption issues; for men it's usually adequate.
- **Magnesium, potassium, zinc** — typically fine if the diet is varied; supplement only with medical guidance.

## Texture and ease of eating

If chewing or swallowing is hard, the goal is "soft and moist" without being baby food.

Texture-friendly meal patterns:
- **Soft proteins**: poached chicken, baked fish, slow-cooked stew meat, eggs, tofu, well-cooked beans, ground meat
- **Tender vegetables**: roasted (well past al dente), braised greens, mashed root vegetables, vegetable purées, soft-cooked carrots, peas
- **Easy carbs**: oatmeal, soft-cooked rice, polenta, mashed potatoes, soft pasta, well-soaked bread (panzanella, ribollita)
- **Moisture matters**: dry food is hard for older mouths. A sauce, broth, gravy, or yogurt accompaniment helps.

Avoid:
- Tough cuts of meat that require sustained chewing
- Hard raw vegetables (skip raw broccoli, raw carrot sticks; use them cooked)
- Sticky or dry breads
- Stringy meats and tough fish skin
- Foods that crumble easily and pose aspiration risk (dry crackers without liquid)

For diagnosed dysphagia, follow the speech-language pathologist's specific recommendations (IDDSI levels). Don't improvise.

## Appetite challenges

When appetite is low:
- **Calorie density beats volume.** Add olive oil, avocado, full-fat dairy, nut butters, soft cheeses to existing dishes.
- **Smaller, more frequent meals.** 5–6 small meals beat 3 large ones.
- **Liquid calories help.** Smoothies with Greek yogurt, fruit, oats, and a tablespoon of nut butter pack 400+ calories in a glass.
- **Make food look appealing.** Color, plating, even small portions on a normal-size plate (not a small one) matters.
- **Eat together when possible.** Social eating consistently raises intake.
- **Address the cause.** Persistent low appetite warrants medical evaluation — depression, medication side effects, dental pain, undiagnosed illness, and dementia all reduce appetite.

## Common medical accommodations

| Condition | Adjustment |
|---|---|
| Hypertension | Lower sodium (under 2300 mg/day, ideally 1500). Use herbs, citrus, garlic, onion, vinegar for flavor. Check labels — bread and processed foods are sodium-heavy. |
| Type 2 diabetes | Consistent carb amount per meal; pair carbs with protein and fat; whole grains over refined. |
| Heart disease | DASH or Mediterranean pattern; limit saturated fat, trans fat; emphasize fish, vegetables, nuts, olive oil. |
| Kidney disease (CKD) | Specific protein, potassium, phosphorus, sodium limits — must be set by a renal dietitian. Don't improvise. |
| Osteoporosis | Calcium + vitamin D priority; weight-bearing activity; protein at every meal. |
| Diverticulosis | Adequate fiber for prevention; if recovering from a flare, a temporary low-fiber phase may be advised. |
| Constipation | Fluid + fiber + activity. Prunes, kiwis, oats are particularly effective. |
| Warfarin therapy | Consistent vitamin K intake (don't have a kale binge one week and zero the next). The amount matters less than consistency. |
| Dementia | Finger foods, predictable meal times, simplified plates, full attention from the eater. |

## Sample patterns that work

- **Egg-and-toast breakfast** with avocado and tomato — soft, high-protein, easy to chew
- **Slow-cooked chicken and vegetable stew** — tender, moist, hits multiple food groups
- **Salmon with mashed sweet potato and braised greens** — soft, nutrient-dense, anti-inflammatory
- **Lentil soup with crusty bread (softened in soup)** — fiber, protein, iron
- **Greek yogurt parfait** with banana and oats — high protein, soft, calorie-dense
- **Frittata with cheese and vegetables** — soft, easy to portion, reheats well
- **Slow-braised lamb shoulder with polenta** — fall-apart tender, rich, comforting
- **Cottage cheese with sliced peaches and walnuts** (chopped fine) — high-protein snack

## What NOT to do

- Do not assume an older adult needs everything pureed. Many do not.
- Do not put older adults on aggressive fat-loss diets without medical supervision. Lean mass is harder to maintain at this age and easier to lose.
- Do not under-protein. Protein under-consumption is the most common nutritional issue in this group.
- Do not over-restrict salt arbitrarily — only if there's a medical reason. Older adults with normal blood pressure don't need a low-sodium diet.
- Do not remove all favorite foods. Quality of life matters at every age.
- Do not ignore weight loss. Unintentional weight loss in older adults is a clinical red flag and warrants medical attention.
- Do not let warm food go cold for long stretches — it loses appeal fast for older eaters.
