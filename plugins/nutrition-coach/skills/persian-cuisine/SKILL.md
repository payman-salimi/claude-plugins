# Persian cuisine

Persian food is built around herbs, slow-cooked stews (khoresh), and a deep tradition of rice cookery. It's heavier on fresh herbs than almost any other cuisine, lighter on heat than Indian food, and richer in fruit-meat pairings (pomegranate with walnut, dried lime with lamb, prunes with chicken) than most cuisines anywhere.

## Core ingredient palette

**Aromatics & herbs (sabzi)**
- Fresh: parsley, cilantro, dill, mint, chives, fenugreek (shanbalileh), basil, tarragon
- Dried: dried mint, dried fenugreek leaves, dried lime (limoo amani)

**Spices**
- Saffron (zaferan) — bloomed in hot water before use
- Turmeric (zardchubeh) — used heavily, in nearly every savory dish
- Sumac (somagh) — finishing acid for kebabs and rice
- Rose petals, dried barberries (zereshk), dried apricots, dried prunes
- Cinnamon, cardamom (in sweets and tea, occasionally savory)
- Persian advieh blend: turmeric + cumin + coriander + cinnamon + dried rose

**Proteins**
- Chicken, lamb (the two pillars), beef
- White fish (mahi)
- Eggs (key in kuku — Persian frittata)
- Beans: red kidney (lubia ghermez), white beans (lubia sefid), pinto, chickpeas
- Yogurt (mast) — eaten alongside almost every meal

**Grains**
- Long-grain basmati rice (the heart of the cuisine)
- Bread: sangak, lavash, taftoon, barbari (whole-grain options preferred)

**Dairy**
- Plain yogurt (mast), feta-style cheese (paneer), kashk (fermented whey)

**Fats**
- Sunflower oil, butter, olive oil
- Ghee (rare in Persian; more Indian/Middle Eastern)

**Acid & finishing**
- Lemon, dried lime, pomegranate molasses (rob-e anar), verjuice (ab-ghoureh)

## Signature dishes

### Stews (khoresh)
- **Ghormeh sabzi** — herb stew with lamb, kidney beans, dried lime, fenugreek (the national dish)
- **Fesenjan** — chicken or duck in pomegranate-walnut sauce
- **Khoresh-e bademjan** — eggplant and tomato stew with lamb
- **Khoresh-e gheymeh** — yellow split pea stew with lamb, dried lime, topped with shoestring fries
- **Khoresh-e karafs** — celery and lamb stew with herbs
- **Khoresh-e morgh ba aloo** — chicken and prune stew

### Rice dishes (polo / chelo)
- **Chelo** — plain saffron-buttered rice with crispy bottom (tahdig)
- **Tahdig** — the prized golden crust at the bottom of the rice pot
- **Zereshk polo** — basmati rice with barberries and saffron, served with chicken
- **Adas polo** — rice with lentils, raisins, dates
- **Baghali polo** — rice with fava beans and dill (eaten with lamb)
- **Sabzi polo** — herb rice (eaten with fish, classic for Nowruz)
- **Tachin** — saffron-yogurt rice cake with chicken layered inside

### Kebabs
- **Koobideh** — minced lamb/beef with onion, on flat skewers, charcoal-grilled
- **Joojeh kebab** — saffron-marinated chicken
- **Barg** — thin lamb fillet kebab
- **Soltani** — koobideh + barg combo plate

### Vegetable & egg dishes
- **Kuku sabzi** — herb-heavy frittata (Nowruz classic)
- **Kuku sib zamini** — potato kuku
- **Mirza ghasemi** — smoked eggplant + tomato + garlic + egg

### Soups & one-pots
- **Aash reshteh** — thick herb-bean-noodle soup with kashk
- **Aash-e jo** — barley soup
- **Abgoosht / Dizi** — lamb-and-bean stew, broth and solids served separately

## Macro patterns

Persian meals naturally lean carb-heavy because rice is central. For a fat-loss plan:
- Halve the rice portion and double the herb/vegetable side
- Trim fat off lamb, choose chicken thigh over leg, fish over lamb on most days
- Save tahdig for once a week, not every meal
- Use yogurt and herb sides instead of bread to round out the plate

A modified Persian plate can land at:
- 40–45% carbs (rice + vegetables)
- 25–30% fats (olive oil, lamb/walnut)
- 25–30% protein (chicken, fish, lamb, beans)

## Technique notes

- **Saffron** must be bloomed: grind ½ tsp threads with a pinch of sugar in a mortar, dissolve in 2–3 tbsp hot water, let steep 10 min before adding to dishes.
- **Tahdig** requires patience — par-boil rice 5–7 min, drain, then steam over a thin layer (oil + thin potato slices, or yogurt-rice paste, or lavash) for 45 min on low.
- **Khoresh** stews want 1.5–2 hours minimum at a slow simmer. Short-cut versions taste short-cut.
- **Dried limes** are pierced before adding, then removed (or eaten by the brave) before serving — they add a deep musky tartness.
- **Fresh herbs** are washed, dried, and used in volume — a single ghormeh sabzi can have 4 cups of finely chopped fresh herbs cooked down.

## Suggested weekly anchors

For a Persian-leaning weekly plan, build around:
- 2 stew nights (ghormeh sabzi, fesenjan, gheymeh, etc.) — each cook covers 2 meals
- 1 kebab night (joojeh kebab is leanest)
- 1 fish night (sabzi polo + grilled mahi)
- 1 vegetable + egg night (kuku, mirza ghasemi)
- 1 simple chicken + rice night
- Lunches: leftover stews on rice, or salad shirazi + bread + feta + walnut + herb plate
- Breakfasts: nan + feta + walnut + tea, or eggs with herbs

## What NOT to do

- Do not skip the saffron blooming — adding dry threads to a dish wastes the saffron.
- Do not call basmati "rice" generically — use real basmati, or the cuisine doesn't work.
- Do not overdo the heat. Persian food uses warmth (turmeric, dried lime, cinnamon) more than chili.
- Do not skimp on fresh herbs. A handful is decoration; cups are the recipe.
- Do not use butter chicken sauce or curry powder. Different cuisine, different rules.
