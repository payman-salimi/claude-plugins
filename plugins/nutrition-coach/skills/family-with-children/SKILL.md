# Family with children

When kids are at the table, the goal shifts from pure macro precision (for the adults) to a balanced plan that's nutritious for growing bodies, palatable to developing taste buds, and not a separate cooking job from the adults' meals. Cooking two dinners every night is the fastest path to the parent quitting the plan.

## Ask first

Before building, get:
- **Ages of the children** (a 3-year-old eats very differently from a 13-year-old)
- **Known dislikes and sensitivities** (texture issues, strong flavors, specific foods)
- **Any food allergies** (be explicit — peanut, tree nut, dairy, egg, wheat, soy, fish, shellfish are the top 8)
- **Whether kids eat the same meal as adults or separate plates**
- **School lunch requirements** (nut-free? heated vs. cold?)
- **The picky-eater spectrum** (mildly selective, moderately, severely / ARFID-territory)

## Portion guidance by age

| Age | Calories/day (rough) | Protein/day | Plate strategy |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1–3 | 1000–1400 | 13–20 g | ¼ adult portion, finger-food friendly |
| 4–8 | 1200–1800 | 19–25 g | ½ adult portion |
| 9–13 | 1600–2200 | 34–45 g | ¾ adult portion (girls) / matching adult portion (boys) |
| 14–18 | 1800–3000 | 46–60 g | adult portion or larger (especially active boys) |

These are rough — active kids and growth spurts push needs higher. Don't restrict calories for children unless under medical supervision.

## Nutrients to prioritize for growth

- **Protein** — at every meal. Eggs, dairy, beans, lean meats, fish, peanut butter (where allowed)
- **Calcium** — milk, yogurt, cheese, fortified plant milks, leafy greens
- **Iron** — lean red meat, beans, iron-fortified cereals, paired with vitamin C for absorption
- **Healthy fats** — fish, avocado, olive oil, nuts/seeds (where allowed), whole milk for under-2s
- **Fiber** — gradually increase via fruits, vegetables, whole grains, beans
- **Vitamin D** — fortified milks, fatty fish; supplement if pediatrician recommends
- **Iodine** — dairy and iodized salt
- **Zinc** — meat, legumes, dairy

## Working with picky eaters

Use these principles, in this order:

1. **No short-order cooking.** One family meal. Kids may eat parts of it; that's fine.
2. **Always include something they reliably eat.** A bowl of plain rice, a side of buttered pasta, or sliced fruit on the table means they will not go hungry, which removes pressure from both sides.
3. **Deconstruct the dish.** Plate components separately on a child's plate. Stir-fry sauce on the side. Tomato sauce in a small bowl. Many kids accept components they reject as a mixed dish.
4. **Repeated exposure works.** A new food may need 10–20 calm exposures before acceptance. Re-offer without pressure.
5. **Cook with them sometimes.** Kids are far more likely to eat food they helped make.
6. **Avoid food rewards/punishments.** "Eat your broccoli or no dessert" creates worse outcomes long-term than just modeling normal eating.
7. **Keep flavors a notch milder than the adult version.** Pull out the kid's portion before adding heat or strong vinegar.

## Severe picky eating / ARFID

If the user describes a child who:
- Eats fewer than 15–20 distinct foods total
- Has visibly fallen off their growth curve
- Avoids entire food groups for sensory or fear reasons
- Has had multi-year aversion patterns

This is beyond meal planning. Recommend they see a pediatrician and a feeding therapist or dietitian who specializes in pediatric feeding. The plan can still help, but it should focus on what works, not on "expanding the diet" without professional support.

## Shareable meal patterns that work for families

These are formats where adults and kids eat the same dish with portion/topping adjustments:

- **Build-your-own taco / wrap night** — kids get plain components, adults get the full topping bar
- **Pasta with sauce on the side** — kids get butter + parmesan, adults get the full sauce
- **Sheet-pan dinners** — protein + roasted vegetables + grain. Kids pick what they want from the tray.
- **Rice bowls** — kids get plain rice + protein + cucumber, adults get full toppings
- **Soup with toast** — kids dunk, adults add chili/herbs
- **Pizza nights** — adults get adventurous toppings, kids get cheese-only
- **Breakfast-for-dinner** — eggs + toast + fruit + sausage. Reliably accepted.
- **Mediterranean meze plate** — many small components, kids pick what they like (hummus, pita, cheese, cucumber, olives, fruit)

## School lunch principles

School lunches need to be: portable, hold without refrigeration for ~4 hours, palatable cold (or in a thermos hot), and nut-free if the school requires it.

Reliable school lunch formats:
- Bento-style: rice + protein + vegetable + fruit, in compartments
- Wrap or sandwich + cut vegetables + fruit + cheese stick
- Thermos: pasta with sauce, fried rice, soup, or stew
- Cold rice noodles + chicken + cucumber + sesame dressing
- Lavash + hummus + cucumber + cheese roll-up + fruit

## Salt and sugar

Children under 1 should have no added salt and no added sugar. After 1, the family meal can use normal seasoning, but be mindful:
- Children under 4: limit added sugar to under ~25 g/day total
- Salt isn't a per-meal target for kids — just keep total daily salt below the family adult guideline
- Avoid honey for children under 1 (botulism risk)

## What NOT to do

- Do not plan a family meal plan that requires cooking two separate meals every night. Find shared meals with separated components.
- Do not restrict calories or fats for children to mirror adult fat-loss goals. Their needs are different.
- Do not give cow's milk before age 1 (it can cause iron-deficiency anemia and is hard on infant kidneys).
- Do not introduce kids to ultra-spicy, very sour, or strongly fermented foods all at once. Build tolerance gradually.
- Do not skip iron-rich foods, especially in vegetarian children.
- Do not weaponize food. Stay neutral about what kids eat at any given meal — repeated exposure works far better than pressure.
