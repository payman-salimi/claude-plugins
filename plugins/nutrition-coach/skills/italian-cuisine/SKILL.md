# Italian cuisine

Italian food is regional. There is no single "Italian cuisine" — there are 20 regional cuisines that differ as much from each other as Persian differs from Indian. Northern Italian cooking (Lombardy, Piedmont, Emilia-Romagna) is rich in butter, cream, beef, polenta, and risotto. Southern Italian (Campania, Sicily, Calabria) is olive-oil-based, tomato-forward, with abundant seafood and pasta. Both are wonderful; they're not the same.

## Core ingredient palette

**Fats**
- Extra virgin olive oil (south, central — primary fat)
- Butter (north — primary fat)
- Lard (traditional southern; less common now)
- Pancetta, guanciale, prosciutto (cured pork — flavor base, not main protein)

**Proteins**
- **Cured meats**: prosciutto di Parma, prosciutto di San Daniele, mortadella, bresaola, speck, salame
- **Fresh meats**: chicken (less central than people think), pork, veal, beef, lamb (Easter)
- **Fish & seafood**: branzino, orata, baccalà (salt cod), tuna, sardines, anchovies, clams, mussels, squid
- **Beans**: cannellini, borlotti, fava, chickpeas
- **Cheeses**: Parmigiano-Reggiano, Pecorino Romano, Grana Padano, mozzarella di bufala, ricotta, gorgonzola, fontina, mascarpone, taleggio
- **Eggs** — common in pastas (carbonara, frittata) and creams

**Carbs**
- **Pasta**: dried (spaghetti, penne, rigatoni, fusilli, orecchiette, linguine) and fresh (tagliatelle, pappardelle, ravioli, tortellini, gnocchi)
- **Rice**: Arborio, Carnaroli, Vialone Nano (for risotto)
- **Polenta**: coarse cornmeal, slow-cooked (north)
- **Bread**: ciabatta, pane di Altamura, focaccia, grissini
- **Pizza dough**: 24–48 hour fermented for Neapolitan style

**Aromatics & herbs**
- Garlic (used with restraint; often cooked then removed)
- Onion, celery, carrot (the soffritto trio)
- Fresh: basil, parsley (flat-leaf), rosemary, sage, oregano
- Lemon zest, red pepper flakes (peperoncino)

**Vegetables**
- Tomatoes (fresh, San Marzano canned, sun-dried, paste)
- Eggplant, zucchini, bell peppers, artichokes, fennel
- Greens: rapini, escarole, radicchio, arugula, Tuscan kale
- Mushrooms (porcini dried, fresh seasonal)

## Signature dishes by region

### Emilia-Romagna (northern, "the food capital")
- **Tagliatelle al ragù** (the real bolognese) — slow-simmered meat sauce, NOT spaghetti
- **Lasagne alla bolognese** — egg pasta + bolognese + béchamel + parmigiano
- **Tortellini in brodo** — meat-stuffed pasta in capon broth
- **Prosciutto di Parma + melon**

### Tuscany
- **Ribollita** — Tuscan bread-and-bean soup
- **Pappa al pomodoro** — bread-and-tomato soup
- **Pasta e fagioli** — pasta and beans
- **Bistecca alla fiorentina** — thick T-bone, grilled rare
- **Pappardelle al cinghiale** — wide pasta with wild boar ragù

### Liguria
- **Pesto alla Genovese** — basil + garlic + pine nuts + Pecorino + Parmigiano + olive oil — over trofie or trenette
- **Focaccia di Recco**

### Lombardy / Piedmont (north)
- **Risotto alla milanese** — saffron, butter, parmigiano
- **Risotto al barolo** — red wine risotto
- **Osso buco** — braised veal shank
- **Polenta** — with mushrooms, sausage, or stew

### Lazio (Rome)
- **Carbonara** — guanciale, egg, pecorino, black pepper. NO cream.
- **Cacio e pepe** — pecorino + black pepper + pasta water
- **Amatriciana** — guanciale + tomato + pecorino
- **Saltimbocca alla romana** — veal + prosciutto + sage

### Campania (Naples)
- **Pizza Napoletana** — Margherita, marinara — wood-fired
- **Spaghetti alle vongole** — clams, garlic, parsley, white wine
- **Parmigiana di melanzane** — eggplant, tomato, mozzarella, basil
- **Sfogliatella** (pastry — for context)

### Sicily
- **Pasta alla Norma** — eggplant + tomato + ricotta salata
- **Pasta con le sarde** — sardines, fennel, raisins, pine nuts
- **Caponata** — sweet-sour eggplant
- **Arancini** — fried risotto balls

## Technique notes

- **Pasta water is a sauce ingredient.** Reserve 1 cup before draining. Add a few tablespoons to almost any pasta sauce — the starch emulsifies the sauce onto the noodle.
- **Salt the pasta water like the sea.** ~1 tbsp per 4 quarts. Pasta absorbs salt only here; under-salted water cannot be fixed downstream.
- **Cook pasta 1 minute under al dente,** finish in the sauce. The pan is the second cook. This is what restaurant pasta does that home pasta often doesn't.
- **Soffritto** — equal parts onion, celery, carrot, finely diced, slow-sweated in olive oil for 10–15 minutes. The base of most braises and ragù.
- **Garlic restraint.** Italian cooking uses garlic, but rarely as the dominant note. A whole clove smashed and removed often beats six minced cloves.
- **Cheese on seafood pasta is a regional taboo** — almost never serve parmigiano with pasta alle vongole or any seafood pasta from Naples south. (Northern exceptions exist.)
- **Risotto needs Carnaroli or Arborio,** not generic rice. Long-grain rice cannot make risotto.
- **Tomato paste should be cooked** for 2 minutes in oil before liquid is added — raw tomato paste tastes raw.

## Macro patterns

Italian food's reputation as carb-heavy is partly true and easily managed. A traditional Italian dinner is multiple courses with smaller portions of each — a fundamentally different rhythm than a single huge plate of pasta.

For a balanced plate:
- Pasta dish as primo (first course) — 80–100 g dry pasta per person
- Protein-and-vegetable secondo — chicken, fish, or beans + a vegetable
- Or: meat sauce + smaller pasta portion + side vegetable on the same plate (Americanized but works)
- Limit cream-based pastas and heavy cheese sauces to 1× per week

A Mediterranean-leaning Italian plate hits:
- 45–55% carbs (pasta or bread)
- 25–30% fats (olive oil, cheese, cured meat in moderation)
- 20–25% protein (fish, chicken, beans, eggs, modest dairy)

## Suggested weekly anchors

- 1 fish-pasta night (vongole, alle sarde, simple oil-and-anchovy)
- 1 bean-and-pasta night (pasta e fagioli, pasta e ceci)
- 1 sauce-anchor night (Bolognese — make a big batch on Sunday, freeze portions)
- 1 risotto night
- 1 grilled or roasted protein night (chicken al limone, fish in cartoccio, pork loin)
- 1 vegetable + grain night (caponata + polenta, ribollita, peperonata)
- Lunches: insalata caprese, tuna + cannellini bean salad, frittata, leftover pasta
- Breakfasts: yogurt + honey + walnuts, or ricotta toast + fruit (Italian breakfast is usually small + sweet)

## What NOT to do

- Do not put cream in carbonara, amatriciana, or cacio e pepe. The egg + cheese + pasta water emulsion is what makes these dishes. Cream changes the recipe entirely.
- Do not serve "spaghetti bolognese." It's tagliatelle al ragù. Spaghetti can't hold a meat sauce — the texture is wrong.
- Do not pair seafood pasta with parmigiano (in most regions).
- Do not use pre-grated parmesan in a green can. The flavor difference vs. real Parmigiano-Reggiano is enormous.
- Do not overcook pasta. Al dente means with tooth — slight resistance in the bite.
- Do not flood pasta in sauce. The pasta should be coated, not swimming.
- Do not use balsamic vinegar in everything. Real aged balsamic is a finishing condiment; supermarket balsamic is best in salad dressings.
