# Nutrition principles

A distilled summary of evidence-based nutrition principles and the U.S. Dietary Guidelines for Americans 2025-2030. Use this as the quick-reference layer; reach for the full DGA PDF in `references/` when you need the source material.

## The five rules that govern almost everything

1. **Calories determine weight; macros determine composition; food quality determines health.** All three matter, in that order, when the goal is body change.
2. **Protein is the most important macro to hit accurately.** Carbs and fats can flex; protein cannot. Under-eating protein costs lean mass, satiety, and recovery.
3. **The best diet is the one a person will actually follow for years.** Flexibility, taste, cultural fit, and social viability beat optimal-on-paper plans that nobody sticks with.
4. **Most people eat 200–500 more calories per day than they think.** Beverages, oil in cooking, sauces, and condiments are the chronic blind spots. When tracking is honest, the math works.
5. **Sustainable rate of change is slow.** 0.5–1% bodyweight per week for fat loss, 0.25–0.5% per week for lean gain. Faster than this is rarely fat-only or muscle-only — it's mixed loss/gain at your expense.

## Calorie estimation

- **BMR**: Mifflin-St Jeor (default); Katch-McArdle when body fat % is known.
- **TDEE**: BMR × activity multiplier (1.2 to 1.9). Self-reported activity is consistently overestimated — when in doubt, drop one level.
- **Goal-adjusted target**: TDEE − 15-20% (moderate fat loss); +5-15% (lean gain). Aggressive deficits (>25%) only for short blocks under medical supervision.

## Macronutrient guidance

### Protein
- General health: 1.2–1.6 g/kg
- Active / strength training: 1.6–2.2 g/kg
- Fat loss (preserve lean mass): 2.0–2.4 g/kg
- Older adults (60+): 1.6–2.0 g/kg
- For overweight clients: calculate from goal weight or lean mass

Distribute across 3–4 meals, ~25–40 g each. Spreading protein across the day stimulates muscle protein synthesis more effectively than back-loading it.

### Fat
- Floor: 20% of calories (hormone production)
- Default: 25–30%
- Saturated fat: under 10% of total calories (DGA)
- Trans fat: avoid
- Omega-3 (EPA+DHA): 250–500 mg/day from fish or supplement

### Carbohydrate
- The remainder after protein and fat are set
- Quality matters more than quantity for long-term health: whole grains, legumes, vegetables, fruit
- Added sugar: under 10% of total calories (DGA)
- Fiber: 14 g per 1000 kcal, or 25 g (women) / 38 g (men) per day minimum

## DGA 2025-2030 — the core dietary pattern

The Dietary Guidelines describe a healthy pattern, not a single diet. The core elements:

**Eat more of:**
- Vegetables (all subgroups: dark green, red/orange, beans/peas/lentils, starchy, other)
- Fruits, especially whole fruits
- Whole grains
- Fat-free and low-fat dairy, including fortified soy alternatives
- Lean proteins, including seafood, lean meats and poultry, eggs, beans, lentils, nuts, seeds, soy products
- Oils — vegetable oils, including those in seafood and nuts

**Limit:**
- Added sugars (under 10% of calories)
- Saturated fat (under 10% of calories)
- Sodium (under 2300 mg/day for adults)
- Alcohol (if consumed: ≤1 drink/day for women, ≤2 for men; less is better; not at all is best)

The DGA emphasizes that healthy eating is a customizable pattern, adapted to cultural, religious, budget, and preference contexts. The plugin's cuisine skills are designed to make this concrete.

## Hydration

- Baseline: 30–35 ml per kg of bodyweight per day
- Add 500–750 ml per hour of intense exercise
- Caffeine and tea count toward daily fluid; alcohol does not

## Micronutrients to watch

The DGA flags these as commonly under-consumed in the U.S. diet. Plans should ensure adequate sources of each:
- Calcium, vitamin D, potassium, dietary fiber (across most age groups)
- Iron and folate (especially for women of reproductive age)
- Vitamin B12 (especially for older adults and vegans)
- Iodine, choline, magnesium

## Special populations

- **Pregnancy & breastfeeding**: increased calorie and protein needs; specific micronutrient priorities (folate, iron, iodine, choline, omega-3); always co-managed with healthcare provider.
- **Older adults (65+)**: higher protein needs; calcium, vitamin D, B12, fluid; texture and appetite considerations.
- **Children**: emphasis on overall pattern quality, not calorie restriction; iron, calcium, vitamin D, omega-3 priorities.
- **Athletes**: higher calorie and protein needs; carbohydrate timing around training; hydration and electrolytes.
- **Vegetarian/vegan**: combined plant proteins; B12 supplementation; iron + vitamin C pairing; omega-3 from algal sources or flax/walnut/chia.

## The behavior layer

Nutrition coaching is coaching, not prescribing. Plans that fail usually fail because of behavior, not biology. Pay attention to:

- **Habit stacks** — pair new behaviors with existing routines
- **Friction reduction** — make the right choice the easy choice (prep once, eat all week)
- **Adherence over optimization** — 80% adherence to a B+ plan beats 40% adherence to an A+ plan
- **Self-monitoring** — tracking, weighing, photo logs raise awareness
- **Social environment** — household alignment, eating-out strategy, holiday plans
- **Recovery from slips** — one bad meal is one bad meal, not a failed week. Get back to the plan at the next meal, not "next Monday."

## When to refer out

Coaches and meal plans are not substitutes for clinical care. Refer to a registered dietitian or physician for:
- Diagnosed eating disorders or signs of disordered eating
- Diabetes (type 1 or type 2 with insulin)
- Kidney disease, liver disease
- Pregnancy and breastfeeding
- Children with growth concerns
- Significant unintentional weight changes
- Persistent gastrointestinal issues
- Multiple medications with known nutrient interactions
